AI adoption feels like déjà vu from the early cloud rush — everyone experimenting, no one documenting, and folks assuming someone else is in charge.

    Your AI isn’t rogue. Your leadership is.

     

    LeadDev’s editor Scott Carey put it perfectly:

     

    “The real AI risk isn’t junior engineers — it’s the execs sneaking prompts into ChatGPT.”

     

    We keep talking about AI like it’s an existential threat. But the scarier truth is that it’s an accelerant for whatever culture you already have. If your org runs on chaos, AI just runs it faster. If your leadership cuts corners, AI makes the corners invisible.

     

    ISACA’s 2026 Tech Trends report states that 84% of professionals admit they’re still beginners with AI, but 98% of leaders call it mission-critical. To me, that sounds like wishful thinking with a budget, not transformation.

     

    AI’s Growing-Up Moment

     

    AI isn’t a weekend project anymore. Ken Johnston, VP of Data and AI at Envorso, described what’s coming next:

     

    “We’re seeing companies build ‘AgentOps’ teams to monitor, train, and govern digital employees like production systems.”

     

    But for most orgs, the maturity curve hasn’t even started. Johnston calls it “the pilot morass” — projects stuck between proof of concept and production, without governance, metrics, or real accountability.

     

    And Ludo Hauduc, Envorso’s CTO, adds the reality check:

     

    “Without proper governance, AI amounts to little more than anecdotal productivity.”

     

    If 2025 was the year of experimentation, 2026 is the year of accountability.

     

    Governance Is Infrastructure

     

    Lots of orgs treat AI governance like paperwork. Mature ones treat it more like plumbing (invisible but essential).

     

    ISACA found that 66% of leaders now list regulatory compliance as their top 2026 focus, followed by business continuity (62%) and AI risk management (48%). With Colorado’s AI Act and the EU’s AI regulations both tightening next year, compliance is an absolutely yes.

     

    Pop Quiz: Who’s Actually in Charge?

    The answer, increasingly, is no one. 

     

    AI is writing marketing copy, approving purchase orders, and setting meetings – all with human oversight that’s often “in theory.” The paradox: the smarter our systems get, the dumber our guardrails become.

     

    Or, as Mary Carmichael from ISACA put it:

     

    “The real risk isn’t AI itself. It’s the widening gap between ambition and readiness.”

     

    Policies built for coders don’t protect you from leaders improvising with sensitive data in public tools. Your next breach might be due to an executive’s shortcut.

     

    CTO Pro Moves

    1. Shift governance upward. Your board needs AI onboarding as much as your engineers do. Build policies that constrain decision-makers, not just developers.
    2. Treat AgentOps like DevOps. If your AI agents are making real decisions, they need uptime monitoring, ethical reviews, and incident playbooks.
    3. Train for emotional intelligence, not just prompt engineering. Leaders who can admit uncertainty will lead safer AI programs than those who pretend they “get it.”
    4. Embed compliance early. Don’t bolt it on post-launch. With regulatory “teeth” arriving in 2026, retrofitting transparency will be ten times harder.
